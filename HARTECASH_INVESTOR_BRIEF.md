# HarteCash — Investor & Sales Brief

**The end-to-end vehicle acquisition platform for franchise and independent dealers.**

## 1. Market Overview

The US used-car market is a **$840 billion industry** with roughly **40 million used vehicles** changing hands each year. Of those, **more than 60% originate with a consumer** who wants to sell or trade in their car. This means dealerships compete for sellers — not just buyers — and vehicle acquisition has become the single most important lever for franchise and independent dealer profitability.

Since 2020, three forces have reshaped the acquisition landscape:

1. **CarMax and Carvana normalized the "instant online offer"** — consumers now expect a 2-minute quote and same-day pickup.
2. **New-car inventory shortages forced dealers to source used vehicles directly from consumers** rather than relying solely on auctions.
3. **Software tools from Cox Automotive and Cars.com consolidated the market** — but their pricing has climbed dramatically, creating an opening for a modern, dealer-first alternative.

The average franchise dealer acquires **40–80 vehicles per month from consumers**. A single additional deal per month at the average used-vehicle gross of $2,000–$3,500 represents **$24,000–$42,000 in annual gross profit**. Tooling that helps a dealer close even 2–3 more deals per month pays for itself many times over.

## 2. Competitive Landscape

### The Acquisition Tool Space

| Competitor | What They Do | Monthly Cost | Key Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AccuTrade** (Cars.com) | Website widget + OBD scanner | **$1,500/mo** (post-acquisition price hike) | Forced ecosystem lock-in; 7x price increase alienated dealers |
| **KBB Instant Cash Offer** (Cox) | Consumer brand offer + lead routing | Per-lead, undisclosed | Offers routinely cut at inspection; leads shared across multiple dealers; customer thinks they're selling to KBB, not the dealer |
| **vAuto Provision** (Cox) | Inventory management + appraisal | **$1,000–$5,000/mo** | Industry standard but focused on post-acquisition inventory management, not consumer sourcing |
| **TradePending** | Website trade-in widget | Undisclosed | Best-in-class widget UX, but stops at lead capture — no pipeline, no customer portal, no check-request workflow |
| **AutoHub** | Service drive appraisal | $49–$299/mo | Affordable but limited feature set; no downstream deal management |
| **CarGurus PriceVantage** | AI pricing intelligence | Undisclosed | Pricing/listing focus, not consumer acquisition; forces dealers to race to the bottom on price |
| **Carmigo** | Dealer-to-dealer auction marketplace | Per-transaction fees | Dealers compete against each other; slow; doesn't solve direct-to-consumer |
| **CarMax / Carvana** | Direct-to-consumer buyers | N/A (they are competitors to dealers, not tools) | They take inventory away from dealers permanently |

### Where Every Competitor Falls Short

Every competitor solves exactly one slice of the problem:

- **TradePending, AutoHub, AccuTrade** → generate leads, then dump them into the dealer's CRM. The dealer is still manually tracking everything after that.
- **KBB ICO** → brings leads, but shares them with competing dealers and puts Kelley Blue Book's brand above the dealer's.
- **vAuto** → helps after the vehicle has been acquired, not during acquisition.
- **CarMax and Carvana** → aren't tools, they're the competition that buys cars away from the dealer.
- **Carmigo** → dealer-to-dealer marketplace that only solves wholesale exit, not customer acquisition.

**No competitor owns the entire customer journey** — from the moment a consumer lands on a dealer's website to the moment a check is cut.
